Building a B2B SaaS financial model isn't just for investors—it tells you if your business is actually viable. Most founders wait until they need to raise capital to build one. That's a mistake.

A solid financial model helps you predict runway, understand burn rate, and identify which levers drive growth.

## Key Components

### Revenue Forecasting: Bottom-Up, Not Top-Down

- **Top-Down:** Estimate market size and assume you capture X%. Don't use this—it's fantasy.
- **Bottom-Up:** Estimate from actual capabilities. "$5k ad spend → 100 leads → 5% conversion → $100/mo customers."

Always use **Bottom-Up**. It forces you to think about unit economics, not wishful thinking.

### Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)

Track every dollar spent on Sales & Marketing:

- Paid ads (Facebook, Google, LinkedIn)
- Content & SEO costs
- Sales commissions + base salaries
- CRM and automation tools

**Formula:** `Sales & Marketing Spend / New Customers Acquired = CAC`

### Churn and Cohort Analysis

Churn is the silent killer. Track both:

- **Logo Churn:** % of customers who cancel
- **Net Revenue Retention (NRR):** Revenue retained from existing customers (upgrades, downgrades, cancellations)

Use **cohort analysis** instead of flat churn rates—different customer groups behave differently.

### Lifetime Value (LTV)

How much revenue a single user generates before churning.

**Formula:** `ARPU / Churn Rate = LTV`

**Healthy ratio:** LTV:CAC should be **3:1 or higher**. Spend $100 to acquire, generate $300+ in lifetime value.

### Forecasting Expenses & Headcount

Your biggest expense is payroll. Model by department:

- Engineering & Product
- Sales & Marketing
- Customer Success
- G&A (Founders, Ops, Legal)

Factor in payroll taxes, benefits (15-20% of salary), and software tools per employee.

### Cash Flow and Runway

SaaS has timing mismatches: you pay annual software upfront but collect monthly subscriptions. Include a **Cash Flow Statement** so you know exactly when runway hits zero.

## When to Rebuild Your Model

Rebuild or significantly update your model when:

- **You launch a new product tier** — pricing changes affect every metric
- **Your churn rate shifts by more than 2 percentage points** — old assumptions are stale
- **You raise a funding round** — new capital changes burn and growth assumptions
- **You switch go-to-market strategy** — CAC typically changes dramatically
- **You cross $1M ARR** — manual spreadsheets start breaking down

Quarterly reviews catch these shifts before they catch you.
